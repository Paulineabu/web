Digital Nursing

Working as a nurse in a digital world

Published: 09 September 2024 1 to 2 minutes

Digital systems can support safe care, or distract from it; the difference is governance, clarity, and good documentation habits.

Digital healthDocumentationInformation governanceClinical safety

Digital systems can help — or distract

Digital tools are now part of daily nursing practice: electronic patient records, e-prescribing, digital observations, remote monitoring, and telemedicine platforms. When implemented well, they improve safety and continuity. When implemented poorly, they create extra work, cognitive overload, and new types of risk.

Where digital strengthens safety

  • Legibility and access: clearer notes and easier sharing across teams.
  • Decision prompts: allergies, interactions, and escalation triggers.
  • Audit trails: visibility of who did what and when.
  • Standardisation: templates reduce variation in key documentation.

Where digital creates new risk

  • Workarounds: staff find shortcuts when systems are slow or confusing.
  • Copy-and-paste: inaccurate or outdated information travels forward.
  • Alert fatigue: too many prompts reduce attention to the important ones.
  • Over-reliance: assuming the system will catch errors instead of clinical vigilance.

Documentation habits that protect patients

Digital documentation should improve clarity, not increase noise. Strong habits include:

  • Documenting clinical reasoning, not just tasks.
  • Keeping records timely (avoid end-of-shift bulk entry where possible).
  • Using structured fields appropriately and avoiding irrelevant templates.
  • Recording safety-netting and escalation decisions clearly.

Information governance in everyday practice

Digital professionalism matters. Key principles:

  • Access only what you need for your role.
  • Use secure devices and approved platforms.
  • Be mindful of conversations and screens in public spaces.
  • Follow local guidance for remote working and telehealth.

Helping systems work for staff

Governance and digital teams should listen to frontline realities. A simple improvement approach:

  1. Collect staff feedback on what slows them down or creates risk.
  2. Identify high-risk workflow points (handover, meds, observations, discharge).
  3. Make small changes (templates, prompts, training, device availability).
  4. Audit and repeat until reliability improves.

What to measure

  • Documentation completeness for key safety elements.
  • Medication error themes and system-related contributors.
  • Time-to-documentation (delays can indicate workflow issues).
  • Staff feedback on usability and burden.

Bottom line: digital nursing is not about technology for its own sake. It is about making safe practice easier, clearer, and more reliable — supported by good governance and strong documentation habits.
