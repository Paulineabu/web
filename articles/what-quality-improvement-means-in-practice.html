Quality Improvement Fundamentals

What quality improvement really means in practice

Published: 04 October 2023 6 to 8 minutes

QI is not inspection; it is structured learning that turns everyday problems into measurable, sustainable improvements.

QI basicsMeasurementPDSAClinical effectiveness

Many people first meet quality improvement through audits or inspections; so it is easy to assume QI is about being checked. It is not. The best QI work feels more like coaching; it helps teams see what is happening, decide what matters, then test changes safely.

When I was leading improvement programmes, the most effective teams were not the ones with the fanciest tools; they were the ones with clarity. They could answer three questions without hesitation; what are we trying to accomplish, how will we know a change is an improvement, and what change can we test.

Measurement does not have to be heavy. A few meaningful measures, tracked consistently, can tell a powerful story. In adult wards it might be deterioration recognition and escalation; in paediatrics it might be safety netting and parental understanding; in community settings it might be follow up reliability and safeguarding pathways.

Public health taught me to look for patterns, not just events. QI brings that habit into everyday care; we look at the recurring themes, not only the one off incident. We also treat staff experience as data; because exhausted people make mistakes, and supported people do better work.

The goal is never a perfect report. The goal is safer care, better outcomes, and a culture where learning is normal.
