Quality Improvement Fundamentals

What quality improvement really means in practice

Published: 04 October 2023 1 to 2 minutes

QI is not inspection; it is structured learning that turns everyday problems into measurable, sustainable improvements.

QI basicsMeasurementPDSAClinical effectiveness

QI is structured learning

Quality improvement (QI) is often misunderstood as inspection, performance management, or “extra paperwork”. In practice, QI is structured learning: teams identify a problem, test a change, measure the impact, and repeat until the improvement is reliable.

QI vs audit vs assurance

  • Audit: checks whether practice meets a standard.
  • QI: changes the system to improve performance.
  • Assurance: provides confidence to leaders and regulators that standards are being met.

These should work together: audits identify gaps; QI addresses them; assurance tracks sustained improvement.

The Model for Improvement (simple, effective)

Three questions guide the work:

  1. What are we trying to accomplish?
  2. How will we know a change is an improvement?
  3. What change can we make that will result in improvement?

Then use PDSA cycles (Plan–Do–Study–Act) to test changes quickly and safely.

Choosing measures that help (not overwhelm)

Good measures are small and meaningful:

  • Process: are we doing the key step consistently?
  • Outcome: is the patient experience or harm reducing?
  • Balancing: are we creating unintended problems elsewhere?

Common QI pitfalls

  • Starting with a solution before understanding the problem.
  • Choosing measures that are too complex or too slow to show change.
  • Testing changes at full scale instead of starting small.
  • Stopping after initial success without standardising.

How to run a practical QI project on a busy service

  1. Pick a problem that matters to staff and patients (high frequency or high risk).
  2. Map the current process (where does it fail, where does it vary?).
  3. Test one change for one shift/day/week.
  4. Review and adapt quickly with the team.
  5. Standardise using prompts, checklists, training, and visual cues.
  6. Sustain with routine audit and feedback.

Making improvement stick

Sustained improvement is about habits and systems. If the improvement relies on memory, it will fade. Build it into the workflow: documentation prompts, safety huddles, standard handovers, and clear ownership.

Bottom line: QI is not a one-off project — it is a way of working. Small tests, simple measures, and reliable systems turn everyday problems into safer care.
