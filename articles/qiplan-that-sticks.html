A QI plan that sticks: from good intentions to measurable change

What makes a QI plan actionable: ownership, small tests of change, measurement that matters, and honest reporting.

Key takeaways

  • Keep aims specific and patient-centred; avoid vague ambitions that can’t be measured.
  • Assign ownership and timelines; improvement without owners becomes background noise.
  • Test small, learn fast: PDSA cycles help teams adapt safely and quickly.
  • Report progress honestly: what changed, what didn’t, and what’s next.

Why QI plans fail (even with good intentions)

Many improvement plans look impressive on paper and then quietly disappear. The most common reason is not lack of effort — it is that the plan does not fit the real workflow. If an improvement relies on memory, motivation, or heroic effort, it will not sustain.

Design for reality: the “friction test”

Before launching a plan, ask:

  • What extra steps does this create for staff?
  • Where will it fail on a busy shift?
  • What makes the safe action the easiest action?

Make the aim specific and measurable

A strong aim statement is time-bound and observable, for example:

  • “Increase completion of falls risk assessments within 6 hours of admission from 60% to 90% in 8 weeks.”

Use a small set of measures

  • Process: % completed within 6 hours.
  • Outcome: falls per 1,000 bed days.
  • Balancing: staff time or delays elsewhere.

Build it into the system

Plans stick when they are embedded into how work is done:

  • Documentation prompts and templates.
  • Visual cues (posters in the right place, not everywhere).
  • Role clarity: who does what, and when.
  • Training that uses real scenarios, not only slides.

Keep feedback quick and local

Short feedback loops keep momentum. Weekly run charts on a ward noticeboard, a two-minute huddle update, or quick sharing in governance meetings is often enough. The goal is not to produce a report — it is to support learning.

Plan for sustainability from day one

Sustainability is a design choice. Consider:

  • What happens when key staff are off?
  • How will new starters learn the process?
  • What routine audit will confirm it continues?

Bottom line: a QI plan sticks when it matches workflow, uses simple measures, and is built into the system — not when it relies on reminders and goodwill.
