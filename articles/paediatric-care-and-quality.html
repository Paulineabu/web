Paediatric care and quality improvement

Published: 12 February 2024 2 to 3 minutes

Paediatric quality is built on safeguarding, communication, and reliability; families need clarity as much as clinical excellence.

Paediatric careSafeguardingFamily centred carePatient experience

Why paediatric quality feels different

Paediatric care often involves uncertainty, rapid change, and complex safeguarding contexts. Quality is built through reliability (doing the right basics every time) and communication (with families and with multidisciplinary teams).

Family-centred care is a quality strategy

Families are not visitors in paediatrics — they are part of the care system. When we treat parents and carers as partners, we reduce errors and improve experience. Practical habits include:

  • Shared understanding of the plan: what we are worried about, what we are doing, what to watch for.
  • Clear medication explanations: dosing, timings, and common side effects.
  • Safety-netting in plain language: when to return, who to contact, and what ‘urgent’ means.

Safeguarding: quality and safety are inseparable

Safeguarding is not an add-on; it is a core safety function. High-quality safeguarding practice includes:

  • Consistent documentation of concerns and observations.
  • Knowing escalation routes and timeframes.
  • Multi-agency communication that is timely and respectful.
  • Awareness of disguised compliance and environmental risk factors.

Reliability: the small checks that prevent big harm

Many paediatric harms are preventable with consistent fundamentals. Examples of reliability bundles:

  • Medication safety: weight-based dosing, double-checks, allergy status, and clear administration documentation.
  • Deterioration: routine observations, paediatric early warning scores, and rapid escalation.
  • Infection prevention: line care, isolation protocols, and parent education.
  • Falls and injury prevention: environment checks and supervision planning.

Communication: where quality often breaks

Common communication failure points:

  • Handover between shifts and between departments.
  • Transfers to specialist centres.
  • Discharge planning and community follow-up.

Practical fixes that work:

  • Structured handover (SBAR) with a short ‘risk summary’.
  • Standard discharge checklist: medication, follow-up, red flags, school/nursery advice if relevant.
  • One named contact for families where possible (especially for complex cases).

Measuring what matters

Pick measures that reflect paediatric realities:

  • Timeliness of escalation for deterioration.
  • Medication incidents (type, severity, contributing factors).
  • Parent-reported clarity at discharge.
  • Safeguarding referrals and response timeframes.

Quality improvement idea starters

  1. Improve discharge information with a one-page family summary.
  2. Introduce a ‘safety huddle’ at the start of each shift for high-risk patients.
  3. Standardise weight and allergy checks on admission and before medication rounds.
  4. Audit escalation compliance and provide feedback in real time.

Bottom line: paediatric quality depends on communication, safeguarding, and reliable basics. When families feel informed and staff feel supported, safety improves.
