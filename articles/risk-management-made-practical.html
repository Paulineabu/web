Risk and Governance

Making risk management practical in clinical settings

Published: 21 November 2025 1 to 2 minutes

Risk registers should reflect reality; the aim is not to record risk, but to manage it with ownership, review, and measurable mitigation.

Risk registerMitigationAssuranceSafety

Risk management should reduce harm — not create paperwork

Risk registers are sometimes treated like a compliance exercise. In reality, risk management is a safety tool: it helps teams identify what could go wrong, decide what matters most, and act before patients are harmed.

What makes a good clinical risk entry?

A strong risk entry is specific and actionable. It answers:

  • What is the risk? (clear, plain language)
  • What causes it? (system factors)
  • Who is affected? (patients, staff, service)
  • What controls exist? (current mitigations)
  • What is the plan? (actions, owners, dates)

Common risk register problems (and fixes)

  • Too vague: “Staffing issues” → define where, when, and what impact.
  • No ownership: actions without names or due dates → assign, track, and review.
  • Not reviewed: risks become historic → schedule reviews and close risks properly.
  • No link to improvement: risks sit separately from QI → align actions with audits and PDSA cycles.

Practical scoring and prioritisation

Scoring is useful only if it leads to prioritisation. Teams can simplify by agreeing:

  • What counts as ‘high consequence’ in your setting.
  • What counts as ‘likely’ vs ‘rare’ based on recent incidents and data.
  • Which risks require escalation to divisional or Trust level.

Mitigations that actually work

Effective mitigations reduce either likelihood or impact. Examples:

  • Standardising handover and escalation pathways.
  • Competency-based training and supervision.
  • Equipment maintenance plans and rapid replacement routes.
  • Clear staffing escalation and cohorting triggers.

Weak mitigations are often “remind staff” or “send an email”. Strong mitigations change the system.

Link risk to assurance

Governance becomes stronger when each major risk has an assurance plan:

  • What evidence shows the mitigation is in place?
  • How often will we check?
  • What data will tell us whether risk is reducing?

What to measure

  • Time-to-action for risk mitigations.
  • Audit compliance for key controls.
  • Incidents linked to the risk theme (trend and severity).
  • Residual risk score over time (is it actually reducing?).

Bottom line: risk registers should be living tools. Keep risks clear, owned, reviewed, and linked to real system changes — that is how risk management becomes practical.
