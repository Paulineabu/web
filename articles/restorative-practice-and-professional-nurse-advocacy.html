Restorative practice and professional nurse advocacy

Published: 03 July 2025 1 to 2 minutes

Staff wellbeing is a patient safety issue; restorative practice helps teams reflect, recover, and maintain safe standards under pressure.

PNARestorative practicePsychological safetyLeadership

Wellbeing is a patient safety issue

When staff are exhausted, isolated, or carrying unresolved stress, safety deteriorates: communication shortens, vigilance drops, and teams become less able to learn. Restorative practice is not ‘soft’ — it protects the conditions needed for safe care.

What a Professional Nurse Advocate (PNA) brings

A PNA supports reflective practice, learning, and resilience through structured clinical supervision. In practice, that means:

  • Creating a psychologically safe space to reflect on difficult work.
  • Helping staff process moral distress and challenging events.
  • Supporting professional growth and confidence.
  • Strengthening team relationships and communication.

Restorative vs. reactive support

Many organisations offer support after incidents. Restorative practice aims to build support before things break. A simple routine can reduce burnout and improve learning:

  • Regular group supervision (monthly/6-weekly).
  • Brief ‘check-ins’ after intense shifts.
  • Structured debrief after adverse events.

How restorative practice supports governance

Governance systems rely on reporting, honesty, and reflection. Restorative practice improves the quality of those inputs:

  • Staff are more likely to speak up early.
  • Learning reviews become more reflective and less defensive.
  • Teams can tolerate difficult conversations without blame.

Practical tools that work on busy units

1) The 10-minute reflective pause

At the end of a shift or after a challenging event, ask:

  • What happened?
  • What impact did it have on you and the team?
  • What do you need now?
  • What should we carry forward as learning?

2) Safety huddles with an emotional check

Add one prompt to existing safety huddles: “Is there anything from the last shift we need to acknowledge before we start?” It normalises reflection without extending meetings significantly.

3) Debrief with clear boundaries

Debriefs are not investigations. They are for staff support and initial learning themes. Keep them confidential, focused, and time-limited, and link learning themes into formal governance routes where required.

What to measure

  • Staff feedback: psychological safety and support.
  • Retention and sickness trends (as part of a wider picture).
  • Incident reporting quality (not just volume).
  • Team engagement in learning activities.

Bottom line: restorative practice strengthens the human side of governance. When staff feel supported and heard, safer care becomes more sustainable.
