AI and Telemedicine

AI, telemedicine, and maintaining quality in care

Published: 18 January 2025 3 to 4 minutes

AI will shape remote care; quality depends on clear triage, escalation, auditability, and human judgement at the right points.

AITelemedicineClinical governanceRisk

Why quality is harder in remote care

Telemedicine removes useful clinical information: you do not always have the same observations, physical examination findings, or contextual cues you would in person. That is not a reason to avoid remote care — it is a reason to be explicit about what ‘good’ looks like.

When AI is introduced (for triage prompts, note summarisation, symptom checkers, or clinical decision support), the quality challenge becomes two-fold: clinical safety and system safety. The question is no longer only “did the clinician make a safe decision?” but also “did the system nudge the clinician or patient towards a safe decision?”

Governance principles that translate well

  • Clear scope: what conditions can be assessed remotely, what must be seen face to face, and what is excluded entirely.
  • Defined escalation: red flags, time-to-review, and who owns follow-up if symptoms worsen.
  • Documented accountability: roles for clinicians, supervisors, and the organisation (including vendor responsibilities).
  • Assurance by design: audits, safety cases, training records, and review meetings that happen routinely (not only after harm).

Triage: make the safe thing the easy thing

Good remote triage is not a long policy; it is a short, usable tool. The most effective triage pathways do three things:

  1. Ask fewer, higher-value questions (the questions that change risk and next steps).
  2. Spot red flags early (so escalation happens before the consultation drifts).
  3. Force a clear outcome (self-care advice, routine review, urgent review, or emergency pathway).

AI can support this by suggesting questions and summarising information, but the pathway must remain clinically owned. If an AI tool is used, your local governance should define whether it is advisory or directive, and what happens when staff disagree with it.

Escalation rules: be specific

Escalation rules should be written like operational instructions, not like a journal article. They should state:

  • What the trigger is (symptom, measurement, safeguarding concern, clinical intuition, deterioration).
  • Who the escalation goes to (same-day clinic, on-call, ED, safeguarding lead, senior nurse/doctor).
  • What the response time is (minutes/hours/days).
  • What documentation is required (what was advised, what was declined, what safety-netting was provided).

Auditability: if you can’t show it, it didn’t happen

Remote care is often challenged later because the documentation is thinner than it needs to be. A simple audit tool improves quality quickly. A strong remote-care note usually includes:

  • Context: why remote, and what information was unavailable.
  • Assessment: symptoms, relevant history, observations available, and risk factors.
  • Decision: what was decided and why (including whether AI tools were used).
  • Safety-netting: what to do if worse, and when to seek urgent help.
  • Follow-up: who will contact whom, and by when.

Bias, exclusion, and public health risks

Remote-first models can unintentionally widen inequality. Public health thinking asks: who is helped, who is missed, and what mitigations are in place? Consider language barriers, low digital literacy, sensory impairments, safeguarding risks at home, and unstable housing. Quality governance here is not just clinical — it is ethical.

Practical steps to implement safely

  1. Create a short clinical safety checklist for remote consultations (triage, red flags, safeguarding, safety-netting, follow-up).
  2. Train staff using scenarios (what good looks like, and what ‘unsafe drift’ looks like).
  3. Start with a narrow scope and expand only when audits show safe reliability.
  4. Audit monthly: escalation compliance, documentation completeness, time-to-follow-up, and adverse outcomes.
  5. Review AI outputs as you would a new clinical tool: error patterns, missed risks, and clinician override rates.

What to measure (keep it simple)

  • Escalation compliance (were red flags escalated correctly?).
  • Time-to-follow-up (was follow-up delivered when promised?).
  • Documentation completeness (especially safety-netting).
  • Patient experience (did the patient feel clear and safe?).
  • Adverse outcomes and learning themes (what keeps recurring?).

Bottom line: telemedicine can be high-quality, but only when the boundaries are clear and the system is designed to support safe decisions. AI can help, but governance must lead.
